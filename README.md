# 2026 U.S. Measles Resurgence — MMR Coverage & Outbreak Analysis

## Purpose

A practice epidemiological analysis project in R (Posit Cloud), investigating
the relationship between declining MMR (measles-mumps-rubella) vaccination
coverage and the 2026 U.S. measles resurgence. Modeled on the same workflow
and documentation conventions as the 2026 cyclosporiasis outbreak project.

**Research question:** How has MMR vaccination coverage changed over the past
several years, and how does that decline relate to 2026 measles case counts
across states?

## ⚠️ Data Source Reconciliation: Three Different "Measles Case Count" Numbers

Any measles case total quoted for 2026 could come from one of **three
distinct CDC/partner data pipelines**, which do not agree with each other
exactly:

1. **NNDSS Weekly Data** (used in this project) — CDC's general
   notifiable-disease surveillance system. This is what
   `NNDSS_Weekly_Data_20260704.csv` comes from. Provisional, weekly,
   subject to backfill (see the reporting-lag pitfall documented in the
   cyclosporiasis project — the same phenomenon applies here: `Cumulative
   YTD` and `Current week` are snapshotted differently and will diverge).

2. **CDC's dedicated Measles Cases and Outbreaks page**
   (https://www.cdc.gov/measles/data-research/index.html) — a
   purpose-built tracker specifically for the current outbreak, updated
   weekly, with its own case-classification and outbreak-attribution
   rules. As of the most recent update: **2,170 confirmed cases**
   across 41 jurisdictions.

3. **Johns Hopkins University (JHU) measles tracker** — an independent
   partner tracker (related to the JHU CSSE MMR dataset used in this
   project) that aggregates state-reported data on its own timing and
   methodology, and typically reports a total a bit different from
   CDC's headline figure.

**Why they don't match:** different update cadences, different
case-classification/confirmation rules, and different timing of when a
case is attributed to a jurisdiction. None of the three is "wrong" — they
are different measurement pipelines answering slightly different
questions. **Do not treat any single number as the definitive ground
truth; when reporting a national total in this project, state which
pipeline it came from.**

Practical implication for this project: expect the NNDSS-derived national
total computed in `01_clean_nndss_measles.R` to run somewhat below the
2,170 figure quoted in CDC's dedicated measles page and in news coverage.
This is an expected artifact of comparing two different CDC data systems,
not a data-cleaning error — confirmed via a built-in sanity check in that
script.

## Known Data Pitfalls (documented as encountered)

- **NNDSS capitalization inconsistency across years**: `Reporting Area`
  values switch between ALL CAPS (2022–2025) and Title Case (2026).
  Matching is done via `toupper()` rather than `str_to_title()`, because
  `str_to_title()` mangles abbreviations like "U.S." (turns "US
  RESIDENTS" into "Us Residents", breaking matches against "U.S.
  Residents").
- **Thousands-separator commas stored as text** in NNDSS numeric columns
  (e.g. `"1,789"`) — stripped before numeric conversion.
- **Flag-value precedence**: a populated numeric value always takes
  precedence over its corresponding flag column; the `-` flag does not
  uniformly mean zero.
- **NNDSS `New York` vs `New York City`** are reported as separate rows.
  Whether the state-level figure already includes NYC or excludes it is
  an open question **not yet verified** against CDC NNDSS documentation.
  This project currently sums the two as non-overlapping totals — flagged
  in `01_clean_nndss_measles.R` as needing verification before NY-specific
  conclusions are drawn.
- **County-level MMR data has state-by-state reporting frequency**, not a
  uniform 8-year panel. Requiring all 8 years of data would silently drop
  29 of 47 states with county data — including Texas, Ohio, and Michigan,
  states central to the outbreak story. Resolved with a two-track
  approach (see below).
- **Arkansas has no county-level MMR data available at all** (no county
  rows exist in the source file for AR).
- **West Virginia has county rows listed in the source file, but every
  one of its 55 counties is blank across all 8 school years** — the
  data dictionary confirms this explicitly: `County-Level 2-Dose MMR
  Data not available` for WV. Functionally the same situation as
  Arkansas (no usable MMR data), but structurally different (WV's
  counties are listed with empty values, rather than absent rows
  entirely) — worth knowing if a row count on the raw file makes WV
  look like it has data when it doesn't. As a result, **the MMR
  snapshot (Track A) covers 46 states, not 47/51** — this is expected
  and confirmed, not a join error.
- **No population weights available** in the MMR county dataset — state
  averages are simple (unweighted) means across reporting counties.
- **Methodology-mismatch flags (trailing `*` in the SY columns):** the
  data dictionary marks specific state/year combinations as not
  comparable to a true 2-dose MMR rate, due to a documented change in
  how that state collected/reported data. Checking every SY column
  (not just the first example found) turned up **three** such
  combinations, all now explicitly excluded rather than silently
  parsed as valid or silently coerced to `NA`:
  - **Louisiana, SY2020-21 & SY2021-22** (128 county-years) — a
    combined vaccine-series rate (DTaP/Polio/HepB/MMR/VAR/Tdap/MenACWY),
    not a true 2-dose MMR rate.
  - **Missouri, SY2019-20** (115 county-years, its entire county set for
    that year) — an old aggregation method later corrected by the
    state.
  - **Utah, SY2017-18** (29 county-years, its entire county set for that
    year) — mixed county-level and school-district-level rates, later
    replaced with a consistent county-level methodology.
  - 272 total county-years excluded across all three. `read_csv()`
    would have silently turned these into `NA` on its own (since the
    asterisked text fails to parse as numeric), but relying on that
    side effect is fragile — it wouldn't generalize to a flagged
    state/year that happened not to have an asterisk. Handled explicitly
    in `02_clean_mmr_coverage.R` via a lookup table instead.

## Methodology: Two-Track MMR Aggregation

- **Track A (Snapshot / cross-sectional):** most recent available school
  year of data per county, averaged to one current-ish coverage estimate
  per state. Maximizes state coverage (all 47 states + DC represented).
  Used for the cross-sectional "does coverage relate to case counts"
  question.
- **Track B (Trend / longitudinal):** restricted to counties with data in
  at least 5 of 8 school years, so that year-over-year comparisons track
  the *same* counties over time rather than being confounded by which
  counties happen to report each year. This includes 35 of 51
  states/territories. States that don't survive this cut (e.g. Michigan,
  Ohio, Indiana — which have at most 1–4 years of county data available)
  are excluded from trend claims, and this limitation is stated
  explicitly rather than implied away.

## Key Findings So Far

**1. MMR coverage has genuinely declined over time — including in every
outbreak state examined in this report.** The Track B trend line
(35 states with data in ≥5 of 8 school years) shows a national-ish
average declining from **0.944 (2019-20, 31 states)** to
**0.905 (2024-25, 34 states)** — roughly a 4-point drop over five years.
More strikingly, **all four outbreak states highlighted elsewhere in this
report show a consistent, monotonic decline with no rebound**:
  - **South Carolina**: 0.973 (2019-20) → 0.951 (2024-25)
  - **Pennsylvania**: 0.971 (2017-18) → 0.932 (2024-25)
  - **Texas**: 0.970 (2019-20) → 0.940 (2024-25)
  - **Utah**: 0.924 (2018-19) → 0.881 (2024-25) — notably, Utah's coverage
    never approached the 95% threshold at any point in the 6 years shown,
    unlike SC/PA/TX which each started above 95% and declined *below* it.
  This distinguishes two different risk profiles: SC/PA/TX represent
  **states that crossed the threshold from above**, while Utah represents
  **chronic below-threshold coverage** — both ended up with major 2026
  outbreaks, but by different trajectories. See
  `output/figures/mmr_trend_chart_20260704.png`.

**2. State-level coverage vs. 2026 case counts: no simple linear/monotonic
relationship.** Spearman correlation between each state's mean MMR coverage
(Track A snapshot) and its 2026 cumulative case count: **rho = 0.014,
p = 0.93** — essentially zero. A follow-up grouped comparison (below vs.
at/above the 95% herd-immunity threshold) also came back non-significant
(Wilcoxon rank-sum, p = 0.55), and counterintuitively the "at/above 95%"
group had a *higher* mean case count (76.9) than the "below 95%" group
(30.9) — driven entirely by one outlier state (see #3). **Conclusion: a
state's statewide average coverage is a weak predictor of outbreak size on
its own.** See `output/figures/coverage_vs_cases_scatterplot_20260704.png`.

**3. Case study — South Carolina shows why the state average is misleading.**
South Carolina's statewide MMR average is **0.951**, just *above* the 95%
threshold, yet it had the single largest 2026 outbreak in the dataset
(686 NNDSS-reported cases; ~1,000 cases per SC's own outbreak-specific
count). County-level detail explains the gap: the outbreak was centered in
**Spartanburg County**, confirmed by CDC scenario assessments, NPR, ABC
News, and SC's own Dept. of Public Health — roughly 95% of the state's
outbreak cases occurred there. In this project's own dataset, Spartanburg's
2024-25 rate is **0.900**, the 3rd-lowest of SC's 46 counties, closely
matching the 88.9% figure reported independently by SC health officials
and multiple news outlets. **This is a concrete, externally-validated
example of how an unweighted statewide average can mask the specific local
pocket actually driving an outbreak.** See
`output/figures/sc_county_mmr_detail_20260704.png`.

**4. Caveat — low coverage is necessary but not sufficient (don't
overclaim).** Two SC counties have *lower* MMR coverage than Spartanburg —
**Jasper (0.825)** and **Fairfield (0.894)** — yet neither had a major 2026
outbreak. This means low vaccination coverage alone does not determine
where an outbreak happens; an actual introduction/index case also has to
land in that specific low-coverage community to ignite sustained
transmission. The right framing is **"low coverage creates the conditions
for a large outbreak if introduced, not that the lowest-coverage place
will necessarily be the one that has one."** This caveat should be stated
explicitly anywhere the Spartanburg finding is discussed, so it doesn't
read as "lowest coverage always drives outbreaks."

**5. Case study — Pennsylvania complicates the story further.** PA's 2026
outbreak is a **two-county cluster**: Lancaster (41 cases) and Lebanon
(~31 cases), together accounting for 72 of the state's 84 confirmed
cases. Checking both counties against PA's statewide average (0.932)
produces two different answers:
  - **Lancaster (0.885)** — 8th-lowest of PA's 67 counties, clearly below
    both the 95% threshold and the state average. Consistent with the SC
    pattern.
  - **Lebanon (0.932)** — sits almost exactly at the state average.
    Despite being heavily affected by the *same* outbreak cluster,
    Lebanon's own coverage number gives no indication it would be
    involved. Confirmed via PA Dept. of Health, WHYY, and Philadelphia
    Inquirer reporting (see Data Sources).

**This means county-level aggregation has the same kind of blind spot
that state-level aggregation does, just one level down.** Lebanon's
involvement likely reflects something not visible in a county average at
all — geographic proximity to Lancaster, shared community/social
networks, or a specific under-vaccinated school or group within Lebanon
that a county-wide number would dilute, similar in spirit to how
Spartanburg's own outbreak was traced to a specific close-knit community
within the county, per NPR reporting. **The general lesson: whatever level
of geography you aggregate to, an outbreak can still be driven by a
sub-unit not visible at that level.** State → doesn't capture county
pockets (South Carolina); county → doesn't necessarily capture
community/school-level pockets either (Lebanon). See
`output/figures/pa_county_mmr_detail_20260704.png`.
